Introduction: cook tonight, stabilise in a week, and get resilient in a month

This guide is a practical, safety-first playbook for Indian kitchens facing an LPG cylinder shortage or refill delays. You’ll: - Cook tonight using whatever power and ventilation you have. - Stabilise in a week by adding a small legal LPG backup or a single induction on a safe socket. - Become resilient in a month with PNG (where available) and a checked kitchen electrical setup.

It’s built for apartments, independent houses, and renters. The priority is legality and safety—so that backups don’t void insurance, violate society rules, or risk fires.

Use this fast map to cook tonight, stabilise in a week, and build resilience in a month—without breaking rules or safety basics.

Keep a window open. - Only 6A sockets or running on a basic inverter (≤1 kVA) → Low-load combo: electric kettle (tea/boil water), rice cooker (khichdi/eggs/veg), microwave (steam/reheat/quick dal). Prefer one-pot meals. - No power but good cross-ventilation/balcony → Single-burner portable gas stove with PESO-approved butane canisters or an OMC-issued small LPG cylinder. Cook with a door/window open. Store canisters cool, away from sun/heat. - PNG serviceable building/locality → Apply now to your CGD; cook tonight using one of the above options.

If you’re renting (no drilling) - Prioritise portable, countertop gear: induction or rice cooker/microwave. If you need flame, pick a 5 kg FTL or 10 kg composite LPG with a compact stove. Avoid permanent fittings.

This week (stabilisation kit) - Fuel: Procure a 5 kg Free Trade LPG (FTL) or 10 kg composite cylinder (lighter, apartment-friendly). - Efficiency: Add a 3–5L pressure cooker and stackable separators to batch-cook rice/dal/veg together. Cook tonight: fast no-gas options (with/without electricity)

If your LPG ran out today, don’t panic. Pick one of these same-day workarounds based on your power and space.

If you have electricity: 6A socket options (no rewiring) - Electric kettle (≤1200W typically suits 6A; 1500W prefer 15A): Boil water for instant poha/upma cups, Maggi, oats, tea. Batch-boil and flask it to speed dinner. - Solo microwave (~700–900W output): Steam veg in a covered glass bowl, reheat rice/rotis, “microwave dal” (pre-soaked masoor/moong + water + salt; finish tadka separately). - Small rice cooker (~300–700W): Set-and-forget rice, khichdi, or steam eggs/veg in inserts. Low-watt, inverter-friendly.

Tonight’s pairings by household size - 1 person: Kettle + instant upma; microwave-steamed veg; curd. 15–20 minutes. - 2 persons: Rice cooker khichdi (one-pot) + microwave papad; pickle/salad. 25–30 minutes. - 4 persons: Rice cooker plain rice + microwave dal (finish a quick tadka on induction/butane if available) + cucumber salad. 30–35 minutes.

If you have a 15A socket: single-burner induction (1400–2000W) - Cook: Rotis on an induction tawa, tadka dal, quick stir-fried sabzi, omelettes. Use flat, induction-compatible cookware and a dedicated 15A wall socket. Avoid thin extension cords. - Sample 30-minute menu: 2–3 rotis/person + tadka moong dal + capsicum–onion sabzi. Cook dal first at medium power, sabzi next, finish with rotis on a preheated tawa.

No power? Use a butane cassette stove—only with ventilation - Where: Balcony or near a window; never in a closed room. Keep combustibles (curtains/tablecloths) away; keep a small extinguisher handy. - Fitment checklist (30 seconds): Cool stove, insert cartridge as per arrows, confirm O-ring, lock lever fully, brief sniff test before lighting (if you smell gas, stop and ventilate). - Cook fast: Pressure-cooker khichdi; dal-chawal; tadka for microwave-cooked dal; rotis on a tawa.

Safety first, always - Ventilation: Open windows/doors for any open-flame cooking. - Clear zone: No loose curtains, paper towels, or plastics near heat. - Quick kit: Heat-proof gloves, a lid to smother pan fires, and a home fire extinguisher (ABC/BC). - Emergencies: Call 101/112. LPG emergency helpline: 1906.

FAQ - Which small appliances work on 6A vs 15A? - 6A: Small rice cooker (~250–500W), many solo microwaves (check input on nameplate), low-watt kettles (≤1200W). One appliance per socket. - 15A: Induction cooktops (typically 1400–2000W) and 1500W kettles. - Is a butane cartridge stove safe in a closed kitchen? No—use only with strong cross-ventilation and a clear safety zone. Shut off and ventilate if you smell gas.

Electric cooking deep dive: induction, microwave, air fryer, rice cooker, OTG

If you’ll buy one device now, pick a 1600–2000W induction cooktop plus a 3–5L induction-ready pressure cooker. It’s the closest drop-in for daily Indian cooking, with high end-use efficiency versus flame.

What each device does best (Indian staples) - Induction (1400–2000W): Fastest for rotis (flat tawa), tadka, boiling, and pressure-cooking dals/rajma/chole. Needs flat, ferromagnetic cookware. Many models let you cap power (e.g., 800–1400W) to avoid tripping weak circuits. - Microwave (700–1000W output): Excellent for reheats, steaming veg, and cooking soaked dals in covered bowls; not good for puffed chapatis. - Rice cooker (300–700W): Set-and-forget for rice, idlis, eggs/veg; great on inverters due to low wattage. - Air fryer (1200–1700W): Crisping small batches (tikkas, cutlets, fries). Not efficient for staple meals. - OTG (1200–2000W): Baking/roasting only; slow and energy-heavy for shortage-focused setups.

Real-world energy use (planning ranges) - Rotis on induction tawa: ~0.03–0.05 kWh per roti (6 rotis ≈ 0.2–0.3 kWh). - Dal (soaked) on induction + pressure cooker: ~0.2–0.35 kWh per 2–3 servings; rajma/chole: ~0.35–0.6 kWh per 3–4 servings. - Rice in rice cooker: ~0.10–0.22 kWh per 1–2 servings (scale roughly linearly). - Air fryer snacks: ~0.25–0.5 kWh per 10–20 minute batch. - OTG sheet-pan roast: ~0.8–1.2 kWh including preheat.

Is induction cheaper than LPG at your tariff? - Electricity cost = measured kWh × your effective Rs/kWh (include surcharges if applicable). - LPG shortcut for comparison: 1 kg LPG ≈ 12.5 kWh of fuel energy. With ~50% burner efficiency, useful kWh/kg ≈ 6.25. Useful Rs/kWh ≈ (Rs/kg) ÷ 6.25. Compare that to your electricity Rs/kWh times your measured kWh per dish. PNG is typically ~9.5–10 kWh/SCM—use PNG tariff ÷ 9.5 to get Rs per useful kWh at 50% burner efficiency.

Inverter/generator compatibility - 600–900 VA inverters: Plan for ~300–600W continuous—OK for rice cooker and low-power microwave use. - ~1 kVA: ~800–900W continuous—insufficient for most induction use. - 2–3 kVA inverter/generator: Can support induction at mid/high power. Always leave 20–30% headroom for surge and other loads. - Rule of thumb: Required VA ≥ (appliance W ÷ power factor ~0.9) ÷ 0.8 headroom.

Wiring and socket basics (before you plug in) - Put induction/OTG on a dedicated 16A/15A socket, 2.5 mm² copper wiring (4.0 mm² for long/hot runs), protected by a 16A MCB and 30 mA RCCB; ensure proper earthing. Avoid power strips/long, thin extensions.

Planner picks - Power cuts common or only a 1 kVA inverter? Start with rice cooker + small microwave. - Stable power and one-device plan? Induction + pressure cooker is your daily driver; add an air fryer later if you snack often. - Building-level load limits? Prefer sub-1000W devices and stagger use.

Wiring and safety: 15A sockets, MCB/RCCB, and earthing

Before you plug in a 1800–2000W induction or OTG, confirm your kitchen circuit can carry the load without overheating or nuisance trips.

Minimum safe setup for a 1800–2000W cooktop - 15A socket on its own radial circuit, wired in copper 2.5–4.0 mm² (use 4.0 mm² for long runs or hot spaces). - Protection: a dedicated 16A MCB for that circuit, plus an RCCB (RCD) at the distribution board (30 mA typical) for shock protection. - Earthing: verify continuity and earth connection (IS 3043 practices). Ask a licensed electrician to test the earth-fault loop/continuity. - Compliance: align with current CEA Safety Regulations and applicable codes (IS 732: wiring practice).

2-minute kitchen circuit check (do this once) - Find a dedicated 15A socket for the cooktop; avoid multi-plugs/power strips or thin extension cords (overheat risk). - At the DB: the cooktop circuit should have its own 16A MCB; an RCCB should be present—press TEST to verify it trips, then reset. - Thermal signs: Warm or browning plug/switch-plate, buzzing sockets, or frequent MCB/RCCB trips → stop use and call an electrician.

Load planning that won’t trip breakers - Don’t share the induction’s 15A socket with fridge/AC. Stagger heavy loads: e.g., 2000W induction (~8.7 A) + 1500W kettle (~6.5 A) ≈ 15.2 A—already at limit. Run them one-by-one.

Apartment/society rules - Some RWAs/time-of-day rules limit high-load use. Check house rules before adding a new 15A circuit or frequently using high-wattage appliances.

FAQ - What wiring do I need for a 2000W cooktop? A dedicated 15A socket on a separate circuit, copper 2.5–4.0 mm², protected by a 16A MCB and an RCCB; verify earthing. - Can I use an extension cord or power strip? No—use a fixed 15A outlet; extension boards/multi-plugs are a common overheating and fire risk at this wattage.

Cost-per-meal calculator: LPG vs PNG vs electric

Use this quick, editable method to compare per-meal cooking costs across LPG, PNG, and electric using your local prices.

Inputs you need today - LPG price per kg: Take your latest 14.2 kg cylinder price ÷ 14.2 (OMC pages or your distributor). - PNG tariff per SCM: City Gas Distribution (IGL/MGL/Adani/Gujarat Gas) tariff page for domestic PNG. - Electricity tariff per kWh: Your DISCOM’s tariff PDF (the CEA Tariff & Duty book links city/state pages). - Meal energy and appliance choice: E.g., pressure-cooker dal+rice, rotis on tawa, reheats in microwave.

Fair, tweakable assumptions - Induction: 80–90% useful efficiency (0.85 default). LPG/PNG stove: 45–55% (0.50 default). Microwaves are efficient for reheats/steaming. - Energy content for conversions: LPG ≈ 12.8–14.2 kWh/kg (use 12.8 to be conservative). PNG ≈ 9.5–10 kWh/SCM.

Method (same idea for all fuels) 1) Decide the cooking job and appliance. For electric, kWh = appliance kW × time (hours). If you have a smart plug/meter, measure once to personalise. 2) Convert to fuel units: - Electric: already in kWh. - LPG kg = (E_food ÷ LPG_stove_eff) ÷ (kWh/kg of LPG), where E_food = E_induction_in × Induction_eff if you used induction to time it. - PNG SCM = (E_food ÷ PNG_stove_eff) ÷ (kWh/SCM of PNG). 3) Cost-per-meal = fuel units × unit price. If you want full-bill realism, allocate fixed charges (PNG/electric) per meal or per month.

Worked examples (edit with your numbers) - Single person, pressure-cooker dal+rice on induction: You measured 0.60 kWh on a 1.8 kW hob (~20 min). At ₹8/kWh, induction cost = 0.60 × 8 = ₹4.8. - E_food ≈ 0.60 × 0.85 = 0.51 kWh. - LPG equivalent fuel energy = 0.51/0.50 = 1.02 kWh → LPG mass = 1.02/12.8 ≈ 0.080 kg. If LPG is ₹65/kg, cost ≈ ₹5.2. - PNG equivalent volume = 1.02/9.5 ≈ 0.107 SCM. If tariff is ₹49/SCM, cost ≈ ₹5.2 (add any minimum/fixed charges pro-rata). - Family of 4, batch-cooking (pressure-cooker stack + one tawa): Total induction input 1.6 kWh at ₹8/kWh → ₹12.8 total, ₹3.2/person. Gas-equivalent fuel energy ≈ (1.6 × 0.85)/0.50 = 2.72 kWh → LPG ≈ 0.21 kg → at ₹65/kg ≈ ₹13.7 total. PNG at ₹49/SCM: 2.72/9.5 ≈ 0.29 SCM → ₹14.2 total. Your local tariffs will shift the winner.

Scenario levers that move the result - Pressure cooker vs open boiling: Open-vessel cooking can raise energy use roughly 20–40%. Batch-cook once, reheat in microwave to cut per-meal kWh/SCM/kg. - Appliance choice: Rice cookers are very frugal for grains; microwaves excel at reheats/soups; induction shines for fast boils and tadka.

Small & composite LPG cylinders (5 kg FTL, 10 kg composite): where and how to buy

If your 14.2 kg refill is delayed, two quick, legal backups are (a) 5 kg Free Trade LPG (FTL) steel cylinders and (b) 5–10 kg composite cylinders via OMCs (Indane/Bharatgas/HP Gas).

5 kg FTL: buy today, cook tonight - Where to buy: Authorised OMC points of sale—IndianOil retail outlets, Indane distributors, selected kirana/supermarkets; BPCL/HPCL fuel stations/distributors in cities that stock minis (availability varies). - Paperwork/KYC: Carry government Photo ID (POI). Address proof isn’t usually needed for 5 kg FTL. Many outlets require you to buy a compatible regulator at first purchase. - Costs: You pay for the filled cylinder and, in some outlets, a regulator and/or a small security deposit. Call the outlet to confirm stock and any deposit before visiting.

10 kg composite: lighter, see-through backup - Why it helps: ~50% lighter than steel and lets you see remaining LPG. OMC-issued composites carry PESO approvals and must meet BIS specifications. - Where/how: Request via your OMC distributor. Some cities allow swapping an existing steel cylinder to composite by paying the incremental security deposit. Call ahead—availability is city-specific. - KYC: Treated like a domestic connection; standard KYC (POI + address proof) through your distributor.

Run-time reality check (family of 3–4) - 5 kg FTL: ~10–20 days for 1–2 simple meals/day using a pressure cooker and lids. Heavy frying/rotis daily can halve this. - 10 kg composite: ~20–40 days under similar use. Treat these as planning ranges; usage varies widely.

Compatibility and safe setup - Use a BIS-marked low-pressure domestic regulator (IS 9798) and BIS IS 9573 (Part 2) domestic/commercial-grade hose. Check the O-ring, use clips, and keep hose length minimal. - Leak check: After connecting, brush a soap-water solution on joints; bubbles = leak. Never use a flame to detect leaks. - Placement: Keep cylinders upright, ventilated, away from heat; turn regulator to OFF after use; in a strong leak, cap the valve, move it to an open area if safe, and call 1906.

Legality: domestic vs commercial - Home kitchens must use domestic-category cylinders (5 kg/14.2 kg and OMC-issued domestic composites). 19 kg cylinders are non-domestic/commercial; do not substitute them in homes unless your OMC migrates your connection compliantly.

Quick tip - Search “how to buy 5 kg free trade LPG cylinder near me” + your city, then call the nearest OMC POS to confirm today’s stock, regulator/hose availability, and any deposits before you head out.

Portable butane/propane stoves: safety, ventilation, and storage

When and where to use - Indoors only with strong cross-ventilation: Keep at least one window and one door open. Prefer balcony or window-side. Never in closed rooms/bathrooms. - CO awareness: If you feel dizzy or have a headache, step out immediately. Consider a CO alarm where feasible.

Setup and fitment (2-minute checklist) - Canister: Inspect for dents/rust/bulging; never use damaged or refilled cartridges. - Align and lock: Match the canister notch to the stove’s locator; engage the lock lever exactly per the manual. Keep the canister bay clean and dry. - Cookware size: Don’t overhang or cover the canister bay with oversized tawas/handis (prevents heat build-up and auto-shutoff triggers). Follow the model’s pot-base limits. - Clearance: Keep 1 m from any LPG/PNG flame or hot appliance. Don’t use wind guards that trap heat around the canister.

Storage in Indian heat and humidity - Temperature is the big risk: Store cartridges in a cool, shaded, ventilated cupboard; never in cars, sun-facing windows, or near cylinders/regulators. Typical guidance: keep under ~40°C; protect from direct sun/heat.

How long does one can last? - A 220–250 g butane can gives ~55–70 minutes at full flame (model dependent). That’s ~1–3 quick meals using medium flame with lids/pressure cooking. Keep a spare, but store properly.

If you suspect a leak or see a small flare-up - Smell hiss/odor? Turn the knob OFF, drop the lock lever, open windows/doors for ventilation, and keep ignition sources away. Don’t operate switches or phones inside; step out and call for help. - Flames small and confined? Turn OFF and let the stove cool; use a lid/damp cloth to smother oil fires—never water. Call 101 if unsure.

Quick don’ts - Don’t use in closed rooms; don’t cover the canister bay; don’t store cans above ~40°C or in vehicles; don’t use damaged/refilled cartridges; don’t place near LPG/PNG flames.

PNG as a stable backup: eligibility, costs, and conversion

If LPG refills are erratic, piped natural gas (PNG) is a stable medium-term fix—metered, on-demand, and billed after use.

Check availability and building readiness - Find your City Gas Distribution (CGD) provider by district/GA on PNGRB’s site; then confirm serviceability for your PIN/locality on the CGD’s site/helpline. - Ask your RWA/maintenance team if a riser already exists and if vertical piping to your stack is planned. If the building isn’t “PNG-ready,” you’ll need society NOC and routing approvals.

PNG connection process (what to actually do) 1) Register online/helpline with your CGD. 2) Submit KYC and occupancy proof (owner: ownership ID; tenant: rent agreement + owner NOC). 3) Pay refundable security deposit and application charges; you’ll get a customer number. 4) Site survey and society permissions. 5) Meter + piping install, leak test, and stove activation. PNGRB’s norms target connection within three months of registration, subject to feasibility.

Costs to plan for - One-time refundable deposit typically up to a few thousand rupees (varies by CGD), with a standard included length of pipe; extra length and civil works are chargeable. - Running tariff: Domestic PNG per SCM varies by city; billing is monthly/bi-monthly. Some CGDs have a minimum consumption threshold per cycle. Compare PNG per-meal cost using the calculator method.

Converting your LPG stove to PNG (don’t DIY) - Most LPG stoves work after jet/nozzle and pressure adjustments. By PNGRB norms, conversion is done by authorised CGD technicians (often included at connection time). Avoid DIY or third-party modifications.

Apartment/society checklist before work starts - Written society/RWA permission for drilling/common-area routing and balcony/shaft access. - Agree meter location (accessible for reading/maintenance). - Align responsibility for tile/paint/restoration where civil work is needed.

Safety and aftercare - Ensure an isolation (shut-off) valve is installed and know its location. Schedule periodic leak checks if offered. Keep the CGD’s emergency number handy. Escalate unresolved issues via PNGRB’s grievance channel.

How fast can you get PNG? - If the network is already on your street and society permissions are in place, installs often finish in weeks. Where network rollout or permissions lag, expect months.

Stretch your fuel: pressure-cooker stacks and batch-cooking menus

Design menus to cut burner minutes first—the fuel you don’t ignite is the cheapest.

Pressure-cooker stacking (dal + rice + veg) - Load soaked dal in the base with 2–3× water; insert rice in a lidded separator above; place quick-steam veg (beans, carrots, lauki) in the top tray. - One whistle on high, then the lowest setting until done; natural release. Finish with a 1–2 minute tadka. Pressure cooking can roughly halve time and energy for staples; separators turn one heat cycle into three dishes.

One-pot staples that save fuel - Khichdi, masala pulao, sambar-rice, poha/upma with steamed eggs. Pre-soak legumes (6–8 hours) to cut cook time. Start with kettle-boiled hot water to reduce time-to-boil on LPG/PNG/induction.

Batch-cook once, reheat fast all week - Cook base dals/gravies together (masoor/moong dal, chana/rajma bases) in one pressure-cooker run. Chill in flat containers; reheat portions in microwave or on induction with a splash of water and a lid. You’ll shift most energy to one efficient cook cycle and sip energy for daily reheats.

Cookware and flame discipline - Use heavy-bottom, flat, induction-compatible pans; lids on; match vessel to burner ring; drop to simmer as soon as pressure is reached. Leverage carryover heat: turn off 3–5 minutes early and keep covered.

Rotis with lower peak load - Partially roast a stack (light blonde) during the main cook. At mealtime, finish puffing on an induction tawa or butane backup in seconds. Keep a damp cloth to soften and a hot box to retain heat.

Mini batch menus (shop once, cook twice) - 1 person (2 cook days/week): - Day 1: Stack dal (moong), rice, mixed veg; tadka later. Portion 3 meals. Breakfasts: poha/upma one-pan. - Day 4: Khichdi with veg + par-cook 6 rotis for 3 meals; finish rotis at serve time. - 2 persons (2 cook days/week): - Day 1: Rajma base + rice + beets in stack; cabbage stir-fry with residual heat. Portion 4 meals. - Day 4: Sambar (pressure) + lemon rice (one-pot). Par-cook ~10 rotis; finish fresh. - 4 persons (2 cook days/week): - Day 1: Chana masala base + masoor dal + rice in stack; blanch beans on top. Portion 2 dinners + 2 lunches. - Day 4: Veg pulao (one-pot) + kadhi (pressure, low simmer). Par-cook 18–20 rotis; finish at table.

Fastest way to boil dal without gas tonight - Soak 30–60 minutes in hot kettle water; pressure-cook on induction/PNG; finish tadka on low. If no cooker, use a tight lid, minimal water, and avoid opening—each peek wastes minutes and fuel.

Semi-urban options: biogas, pellet, and solar cookers

If you have a small yard/terrace or a well-ventilated balcony, these non-LPG options can keep staples going during shortages. Here’s what’s realistic and compliant.

Biogas (household-scale) - Feasibility: Works if you can feed it daily with segregated kitchen waste and/or dung. A ~2 m³/day plant can meet many five-person families’ needs; smaller prefabs exist but still need consistent feed and sun-warm conditions. Plan an outdoor patch and slurry handling. - What it cooks well: Steady, medium flame—pressure-cooked dal/chawal, sambar, slow simmers. Quick high-heat tadka is better finished on a separate flame. - Maintenance: Daily feed, occasional desilting; manage slurry as fertiliser; expect lower yield in cool/rainy spells. Provide drainage and mosquito-proofing. - Compliance tip: Choose a biogas stove/burner conforming to BIS IS 8749; use BIS-marked hoses/regulators where applicable.

Pellet/biomass stoves (balcony/yard only) - Heat profile: Strong, short-burst heat for rotis, tadka, and quick sabzi. Forced-draft designs generally cut PM/CO versus natural-draft, but emissions are not zero—never use in closed kitchens. - Balcony usability: Only with society/neighbor consent and cross-ventilation. Expect some plume/odour; add a wind baffle and ash tray. Prefer models tested to BIS IS 13152 or ISO 19867-like protocols.

Solar cookers (terrace-first backup) - When it works: Best on clear days for rice, dal, khichdi, and slow-cook dishes; plan 1–5 hours depending on sun. Monsoon/cloud cover will stretch or halt cooking. Treat solar as a fair-weather supplement. - Access: Unobstructed terrace time is ideal; balcony use is often too shaded. Secure parabolic units against wind.

Standards and safety shorthand - Look for: BIS IS 13152 (portable solid biomass cookstove) and BIS IS 8749 (biogas stove). For gas components, use BIS-marked LPG hose (IS 9573) and PESO-approved regulators/valves. These checks reduce leak/fire risks and help with society approvals.

Quick answers - Is a small biogas unit realistic? Yes—if you can feed it daily and have an outdoor patch; size appropriately and accept seasonal yield swings. - Are pellet stoves OK on balconies? Only forced-draft, outdoors, and well-ventilated; expect some smoke/PM and get society consent. - Solar vs induction as backup? Solar is free on sunny days but unreliable in monsoon/evenings; induction is the dependable anytime backup (subject to electricity supply).
